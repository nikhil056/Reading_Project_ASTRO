Binary Systems in Astrophysics

  • Many of the stars belong to binary systems. As a result, it is critical to comprehend the distribution of stellar masses at birth, the number of stars born not only as singles but also as binary systems, and the mass ratio and orbital period distribution of these binary systems.
  • This is especially true for eclipsing binaries, which are binary stars with our line of sight in the binary's orbital plane. Additionally, as is the case in interacting binaries, each component in the binary may be at a distinct stage of development, which may, in turn, be altered by the companion's presence. This information is critical for constraining and testing theoretical models of stellar structure and evolution.
  • Binary systems are worth studying because of the sheer richness of the various classes of binaries, as well as the fact that these objects reveal a great deal about themselves. Binaries are classified according to their component stars, such as Cataclysmic Variables are variables that have the potential to cause a cataclysm.

Each of the mentioned classes of binary stars offers the opportunity to study stars in an extreme environment which is impossible to replicate in man-made laboratories. Accretion due to Roche lobe overflow is a crucial phenomenon in stellar astrophysics (Frank et al., 2002), as it alters the fate of binary systems and produces distinct observational effects that allow us to observe these objects in the first place. The formation of an accretion disk around the accreting star is unavoidable due to accretion until the accretion stream impacts the accreting star's surface. The accretion disk is found in protoplanetary disks, active galactic nuclei (AGN), and binary stars. The modeling of accretion disks provides a comprehensive understanding of astrophysical objects and their associated observations. Binaries are important to astrophysicists because they are the most accessible of all objects containing accretion disks.

The Roche Model

Because there is a point between the two components where effective gravity vanishes and matter from one star can flow freely toward its partner, the evolution of a binary star differs from that of a single star with the same mass and chemical composition.

To understand the Roche model, it is necessary to understand Circularization and Synchronization.

Small eccentricities are found in binaries with shorter periods (on the order of days), intermediate masses, and components with sizes corresponding to orbital separation. In contrast, binaries with a big separation have larger eccentricities. When it comes to their orbital revolution, the components' spins appear to be coordinated.

In shorter period binaries Circularization and synchronization is common because the mechanical energy of a binary is lowest when both stars have circular orbits and synchronized spins for given masses and fixed angular momentum. Because all physical systems tend to such a stable equilibrium, binaries will tend to achieve circularization and synchronization.

From the above discussion, it can be concluded that binaries with initial periods of days and a small initial eccentricity will reach their equilibrium configuration (synchronized and circularized) in a time scale shorter than their evolutionary time scale In the case of binaries with longer initial periods and/or large initial eccentricities, will they become circularized or not? is subject to more research.

Discussion on the roche model

The condition is that circularization and synchronization are maintained throughout the RLOF process. Tidal interaction will keep the orbit stable. A saddle point (zero effective gravity point) exists between the two components even when the primary envelope rotates asynchronously to the orbit.

A critical surface from which funneled mass loss occurs can also be defined. The critical radius differs only slightly from the Roche radius calculated as if the star were synchronized for spin periods close to the orbital period. Even in binaries with eccentric orbits, there is a saddle point between the two components.

Because its precise location is determined by the orbital phase, a situation may arise in which mass loss by the critical lobe is not constant in a single revolution, but exhibits on-and-off phases. As a result, binaries may evolve through two mass exchange stages: a rapid mass transfer phase in which the mass ratio of the two components is reversed from q > 1 to q 1 (q = M1/M2), followed by a gradual increase in the secondary's mass M2. The rapid phase can last as little as 104 to 105 years. As a result, the following events occur during mass transfer:

• A-B: On a nuclear timescale, star 1 expands.

• B-C: On a thermal timescale, rapid mass transfer occurs. The system is made up of a subgiant (the primary's remnant, now the less massive star) and a main sequence star (the most massive star).

• Beyond C, the subgiant evolves on a nuclear time scale, slowly expanding and transferring matter.

Conservative and Non-conservative Evolution

We can consider the following options for computing evolution sequences:

a) Conservative evolution: total mass and angular momentum are assumed to be constant. As the mass transfer begins, mass is transferred from the primary to its companion. The variation of the period and the resulting variation of the Roche radius can be derived from the mass change of the two components.

b) Non-conservative evolution takes into account mass, angular momentum losses, contact phases. Details are required to define the fraction of lost mass that was not accreted by the secondary and thus left the system, as well as momentum losses, which were stored in a disc or a common envelope. In both cases, the computations can be limited to the evolution of the primary and use the changing mass of the secondary only to calculate the changing period, or both components can be calculated simultaneously. Non-conservative treatment must account for mass and angular momentum losses.
